02

The vetter's mindset

Checking a merchant's licensing early avoids wasted work. Picture 25 minutes spent writing a polished hair-salon deal — images sourced, title locked, fine print drafted — then you check the licensing and it's expired. 25 minutes, gone.

The root cause: vetting and writing happened in the wrong order. The writing work was built on top of something that hadn't been verified.

Vetting is a gate, not a quality check. It catches the showstoppers before writing happens — so your writing work is never built on a deal that can't ship.

A useful edge case: some issues feel like writing/fine-print (e.g. a "Buyer Max" parameter) but actually belong in vetting, because setup parameters need verifying against the contract before you write. When in doubt, treat it as vetting: check the contract yourself, or IR Sales for clarification.

3·1

Check 1 — DAC7 / DSA

Scenario

A new submission lands: Italian restaurant, new account on the EU platform.

DAC7 is an EU directive requiring marketplaces to collect seller tax information. DSA (Digital Services Act) covers consumer disclosures. Together, they're the legal floor for new merchants. If the field isn't right, we can't ship.

Two classic traps. 1) DAC7/DSA applies regardless of category — it's about the merchant's location and account status, not the type of deal. 2) We never tick the "Self-Certified" box on the merchant's behalf: it's a self-attestation only the merchant can make. And don't reject — the merchant can resolve this with Sales: we IR, we don't reject.

The procedure, every new merchant

Open the correct Salesforce recordThe merchant's Account — not the Opportunity. DAC7: EU, UK, Canada, Australia. DSA: EU Feature Countries excluding the UK.
Check the "DAC7/DSA Info" fieldThe status must read "Submitted" or "Verified."
Validate self-certificationFor EU Feature Countries, confirm the "DSA/DAC7 Self-Certified" checkbox is ticked.
Act if information is missingSend an IR to the Opportunity Owner. If the parent account is already "Verified," the child account can still be vetted by manually setting its status to Verified.
A new EU merchant's SF account shows DAC7/DSA Info: "Invalid." What's your action?
→ IR the Opportunity Owner. The deal can't move forward until DAC7 is Submitted or Verified. DAC7 is a legal floor for new EU/UK/CA/AU merchants. The fix is on Sales' side: we IR, we don't ship, we don't tick the box, we don't reject.
3·2

Check 2 — Pricing

Scenario

A new local massage deal lands. The Unit Value in Salesforce doesn't match the price published on the merchant's website.

A mismatched Unit Value either overstates the customer's savings (legal / refund risk) or contradicts what the merchant publishes (trust risk). The vetter's job: catch this before a customer sees it.

Your pricing check, every time

Check the price in SalesforceOpen the deal and note the Unit Value for each option.
Check the merchant's websiteFind the same service or offer.
CompareSF Unit Value vs the published website price.
If they matchContinue vetting the deal.
If they don't matchAlign the SF value to the website, then: screenshot the price → attach it to the opportunity → IR the Account Owner → add #valueadjusted to Context Notes.
If there's no website priceLook for a Proof of Pricing document on the opportunity or account.
Metro-specific: after adjusting, check the discount against the thresholds sheet. If the discount stays within range → continue with #valueadjusted. If it falls outside range → do NOT use #valueadjusted; send a Follow-Up with the Pre-Discounted Value Doesn't Match Website tag.
The website shows €60, the SF Unit Value shows €75. You adjust SF to €60. Next step?
→ IR the Account Owner about the value change + add #valueadjusted to Context Notes. Sales needs to know we adjusted, and the hashtag flags it for downstream audit. Without these two steps, the change is invisible to the rest of the team.
3·3

Check 3 — Licensing

Scenario

You're vetting a Local deal — laser hair removal, new merchant, standard PDS. The PDS status in the LVG reads "LP."

The LVG (Legal Vetting Guidelines) is your single source of truth for licensing by PDS. It's where Legal tells you which PDSes are banned, which need verification, and which are fine to vet through. You'll come back to it often.

Licensing check, by LVG status

Status in the LVGWhat you do
BlankNo license check. Continue with the other checks.
LP (Legal Parameters)Run the License Check. For Massage PDSes: also Online Reviews Check + Location Check.
DNC / Ban (Metro)Reject with the DNC tag.
Local edge cases: for deals with more than 3 locations, you can accept proof of a staff-qualification policy instead of individual licenses. Exception: aerial sports and leisure flights always require licensing.
If a PDS isn't covered by the SOP or LVG: no explicit license requirement → you can vet without a license check; no entry in the eligibility lists → the PDS is eligible by default. When in doubt, flag the gap via the Asana form — don't invent a rule.
A merchant's submitted license has expired. Which Invalid Data tag?
→ "Provided Licensing has Expired." Not to be confused with "Required Licensing is Missing" (= the merchant uploaded no license at all). Tag precision is what makes the merchant's path forward clear.
3·4

Check 4 — PDS Eligibility (Metro only)

Scenario

A Metro deal lands with 2 options: Option 1 — 60-min Swedish Massage; Option 2 — Cosmetic Botox Injection. Massage is Metro-eligible. Injectables are not.

The Metro flow is built for self-service. If a deal can be salvaged, we salvage it. We only reject when the entire deal can't run on Metro — at which point we route the merchant to a Sales rep.

Two cases, two actions

SituationAction
Some options eligible, some notFOLLOW-UP with the "Deal Details" tag. Clearly state which option(s) to remove.
The entire deal isn't Metro-eligibleREJECT with "Not Self Service PDS."
Critical order. Reject FIRST, update the PDS SECOND. If you change the PDS before rejecting, the merchant doesn't see the right rejection message — they're left confused, submitting as PDS X and getting rejected as PDS Y.
All 3 options on a Metro deal fail eligibility. What's the sequence?
→ Reject with "Not Self Service PDS" first, THEN update the PDS. Order matters. Reject first gives the merchant a clear message and routes them to a Sales rep.

Check 5 — Merchant website (Metro only)

Fast check, yes/no rule. On Metro: the merchant's website must work AND its content must be relevant to the deal. Rep-signed deals come with a vetted Sales handshake — no website check needed.

Website check (Metro)

04

The tag language

Tagging is how you talk to the merchant — and to your future self. The right tag tells the merchant exactly what to fix, and tells the next agent what's already been flagged.

FOLLOW-UP Pre-Discounted Value Doesn't Match Website

Use when the regular price in SF is higher than the regular price on the website. The ECW must include both prices: the Regular Price entered in Salesforce and the one found on the website (or Proof of Pricing).

Sample message: "We see the Regular Price on your website is €80, while the Regular Price entered in the deal is €120. Please amend the deal's Regular Price to match the website, or provide an updated Proof of Pricing showing the higher amount."

FOLLOW-UP Service(s) Not Listed on Merchant's Website (Metro)

When the site doesn't list the services included in the deal. Sample message: some of the options in the Groupon deal aren't available on the website; please list the services and prices on the site, or provide a valid Proof of Pricing.

REJECT DNC

A blanket ban determined by Legal — the PDS is banned site-wide, listed in the LVG. No path forward for this merchant on this PDS. Rejection is final.

REJECT Not Self Service PDS (Metro)

The PDS can run as a non-Metro deal but not through self-service. Critical order: REJECT first, then update the PDS. The merchant is redirected to a Sales rep for non-Metro deal creation.

4·1

Fixing a tag on a Metro deal

The rule that shows up in the assessment. Changing a tag on a Metro deal isn't a quick edit. Without the three-step procedure, the merchant still sees the old rejection message — confusing for them, expensive for us.

The three-step procedure

Move the deal to "Rep Getting Info"Change the status, then save.
Move it back to "Recommended"Reset the status, then save.
Update the Invalid Data tagSelect the correct tag, write your message in Editorial Communication/Warnings (ECW), save.
Any time a deal moves between A Sure Thing and Rejected, send an email via SF to inform the merchant. When moving to A Sure Thing, remove the Invalid Data tag from the opportunity.
You'd tagged "Required Licensing is Missing." The real issue is a value mismatch. What do you do?
→ Rep Getting Info → Save → Recommended → Save → change the tag to "Pre-Discounted Value Doesn't Match Website" + update the ECW with both prices → Save. This is the only way the new message actually reaches the merchant. Skipping the steps leaves the old message showing — which is wrong and erodes trust.

Goods INTL — specialised vetting

Goods INTL adds its own flow on top of the 5 core checks. The big deltas: brand-tier verification, an Apple-specific approval flow, and a refurbished rule that's the opposite of NAM Gateway's.

Scope. This module covers Goods INTL. NAM Goods deals run through Gateway — a different tool and flow — and stay with Murali's team; they're NOT covered here. If you're ever asked to vet NAM Goods → escalate.
Scenario

A Goods INTL deal lands: Apple AirPods, UK-based vendor. New merchant, new account. The vendor is not on the Apple-approved list.

Brand-tier check, every Goods INTL deal

SituationAction
UnbrandedStandard checks. No tier approval needed.
Branded T2 / T3, or exempted vendorVet the deal through.
T1 brandRequires an approval recorded by an authorised approver OR a provenance document attached to the deal.
Apple (own track)Vendor on the Apple list → vet through. Not on the list → legal hold, Sales obtains supply-chain provenance, Legal verifies, vendor gets added, then the deal proceeds.

Exempted vendors: BVG Airflo, James Russell, E.com International (Home & Garden only), UPGS, Van Meuwen.

AirPods lookalikes: any non-Apple product styled to look like AirPods → flag to Legal for IP infringement. Do not vet through as "Apple."

Refurbished — the inverted rule

If the refurbishment grade (A / B / C) is MISSING, you do NOT reject. You send an IR to Sales asking them to confirm the grade. The deal sits in Rep Getting Info until Sales confirms — the body copy must mention the grade before launch.

This is the OPPOSITE of NAM Goods Gateway, where a missing grade = automatic rejection. INTL flags-and-holds; Gateway blocks outright.

Four more rules worth knowing for Goods INTL

TopicRule
UK T1 brand pricingIf the brand is on the Comparable Brands list AND is T1, the Proof of Pricing must come from a Reputable Retailer or the manufacturer's website. (Refurbished: vendor PV is acceptable even for T1.)
Cross-border shipping proofIf the merchant's billing country differs from the deal's feature country, shipping proof is required (basket screenshot, T&Cs, signed letter from a company director, or SHPRFOK in Logistics notes). Same-country deals need no proof.
Food / Cosmetic / DetergentNeed additional checks (see the QA check matrix). Tablets and liquid medicines aren't "food" → no Food Doc. Protein shakes ARE "food" → Food Doc required.
MRT AttributesIgnore for vetting. Not visible to Sales before approval and not reliably verifiable. Don't raise IRs on MRT mismatches.
Goods INTL = the 5 core checks + the brand-tier flow + the refurbished-IR rule + the 4 quick references. Most Goods INTL deals vet in under 7 min once the tier check becomes reflexive.

Appendix — notes by Line of Business

Reference, not assessment. Reach for it when you vet outside Local/Metro. The 5 core checks all still apply — the appendix only flags what's different.

National / Enterprise / Mid-Market

DAC7: same (INTL excluding UAE + Canada, new merchants only, at the Account level). PDS Eligibility / Licensing: same as Local. Pricing: same as Local (Unit Value vs website). Carve-out: for multi-location deals, you can accept a staff-qualification policy instead of individual licenses — EXCEPT aerial sports and leisure flights (licensing always required). Scheduling: agents do NOT schedule National / Mid-Market deals — Sales handles it.

GrouponLive

DAC7: same as National. Licensing: the LP path requires License Check only — no Online Reviews / Location. Pricing is more lenient: if pricing isn't on the site and the PV doc doesn't meet guidelines, push the deal through WITHOUT raising an IR. Scheduling: NAM Non-DCT GLive → Sales schedules; INTL → standard "A Sure Thing" rules.

Travel

DAC7: AU exemption — international travel on the AU platform with redemption outside AU is exempt (mark "Verified" and note the reason in RRDP Info Submitted by). Pricing: no price check for Booking deals; Tour Operator deals are validated via the submission doc attached by Sales (IR if missing or if salient features don't match). Property deals: price match against the website required (Groupon price can't be higher). Scheduling: NAM Travel → Sales; INTL Voucher → A Sure Thing once feature dates exist; INTL Extranet → A Sure Thing then schedule once Booking inventory = "Extranet Approved."

UAE (across LoBs)

MOH (Ministry of Health) — AE medical/cosmetic deals (Botox, fillers, dental, injectables, aesthetic procedures): the MOH Advertising License is requested by the Site Editor, not the Vetter. MOH UAE deals: do NOT manually schedule (confirm it's schedulable, leave scheduling to Sales). DAC7: UAE is EXCLUDED from the DAC7/DSA check. Images: same-gender only in medical imagery; no displays of affection; no one drinking/touching alcohol (except bartenders/servers); limited visible skin.

Not in this course: NAM Goods (Gateway-based) — a fundamentally different tool and flow (different submit mechanic, inverted refurbished rule = reject vs IR, no permalink management). Owned by Murali's team. If you're asked to vet it → escalate.

08

Recap & golden rule

Vetting isn't about checking boxes. It's about protecting launch quality, reducing operational risk, and giving merchants a smoother path to go live.

1 · Verify before you write

Always the 5 core checks first. A deal that fails vetting never moves into writing.

2 · Tag with accuracy

Every Follow-Up / Reject / Invalid Data tag drives merchant communication. Right tag, right workflow, clear ECW.

3 · Flag, don't guess

When something's unclear, pause and escalate via the SOP, your TL, or the Asana form. Confidence comes from verified decisions, not assumptions.

Three things before you start

• Bookmark the Module 5 references — SOP, LVG, PDS Eligibility, Asana form are the four you'll use most.

• Your first five live deals: pair-vet. Don't go solo on day one. The flow becomes reflex faster when you talk it through out loud.

• Something unclear or a deal you're stuck on? Ping your TL first, then the Asana form if it's a missing rule. No email workarounds.

The golden rule, worth repeating: "If you can't remember the rule, look it up. If you can't find the rule, flag it." The only wrong move is the silent one — flag-and-escalate is always safe.
